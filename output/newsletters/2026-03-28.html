Market News

Saturday's market close delivered a pair of structural milestones that shift the framing from a rough week to a genuine bear phase. The Nasdaq 100 and Dow Jones Industrial Average both officially entered correction territory, and the S&P 500 logged its fifth consecutive weekly decline — its longest losing streak since 2022 — with the index down 1.67% Friday, the Dow shedding 793 points, and Nasdaq off 2.15%. The selling is no longer purely an Iran/energy story. Two separate pressure vectors are converging: rising Treasury yields are compressing equity multiples even as risk sentiment deteriorates, and a new tariff regime has quietly reshaped the trade law landscape. The Supreme Court struck down IEEPA-based tariffs earlier this quarter, forcing the administration to pivot to Section 122 of the Trade Act for authority, resulting in a flat global 10% import tariff structure. That reset introduces a different legal durability — Section 122 tariffs are harder to challenge quickly — and emerging markets are repricing accordingly as dollar liquidity tightens and import cost structures shift. Credit markets are beginning to show stress independent of equity moves, which is the signal worth watching most closely as April opens; equity corrections can reverse fast, but credit spread widening tends to be stickier and has historically preceded deeper drawdowns when it appears alongside oil shocks.

The week ahead — March 30 through April 3 — will be the first full trading week with all of these dynamics fully priced in by sophisticated participants. Macro calendar items include any Fed speaker commentary on the inflation revision and tariff pass-through, which will set tone early. The key risk is that the Iran situation and tariff regime are now being treated as durable structural inputs rather than headline noise, meaning the short-volatility / buy-the-dip reflexes that dominated 2023-2025 are less reliable here.

Project Applicabilityanalysis

The two developments with direct bearing on this stack are MemoryAgentBench and the Cursor Automations event-driven pattern — and they hit opposite ends of the same problem. On the memory side: MarketSwarm, SportsBettingSwarm, and the Kalshi trader all run as stateful multi-session agents, but there has been no rigorous way to measure whether they are actually retaining and applying context across rounds. MemoryAgentBench from ICLR 2026 is the first standardized yardstick for exactly that capability gap. The AlgoSwarm IS/OOS gap — 36.2% structural divergence — is partly a regime-awareness problem; agents that fail to carry forward learned context about market conditions across sessions will keep overfitting. Running the swarm workflows against MemoryAgentBench-style evaluation tasks would expose where memory is degrading and give a concrete score to optimize against, rather than inferring it indirectly from backtest drift. On the event-driven side: the DailyBriefing pipeline and KalshiTrader both still depend on Windows Task Scheduler for triggering — a polling model that forces fixed cadence regardless of whether anything meaningful has changed. The Cursor Automations pattern (fire on external webhook, price event, or platform notification) is exactly what a market-reactive Kalshi agent needs. A price threshold crossed, a new market listed, or a settlement event should trigger the trading logic directly, not wait for the next scheduled poll. The pattern is now validated at production scale by Cursor and Sourcegraph — the infrastructure to receive those event payloads already exists via the MCP message bus.

Recommendations
Benchmark MarketSwarm and SportsBettingSwarm memory handling against MemoryAgentBench evaluation tasks — the ICLR 2026 paper is the first rigorous yardstick for cross-session context retention, and the IS/OOS structural gap in AlgoSwarm is a plausible symptom of agents losing regime context between rounds. Run the benchmark before the next swarm optimization pass so improvements have a measurable target rather than relying on backtest delta as a proxy.
Replace Windows Task Scheduler triggers in KalshiTrader with event-driven execution — wire the existing MCP message bus or FastMCP layer to fire on market events (new listing, price threshold crossed, settlement) rather than polling on a fixed cron. The Cursor Automations architecture is now production-validated; the infrastructure is already in place.

The framework selection question is settled enough that a time-boxed spike comparing it against the custom WAT framework is worth scheduling before the next major swarm refactor.
